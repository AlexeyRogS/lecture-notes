3.3 Subsets of the Real Line

It is helpful to note at this point that the completeness property from before also guarantees the existence of infimums for non-empty subsets that are bounded below.

Theorem 3.3

Every non-empty subset of ℝ that is bounded below has an infimum in ℝ.

  • Proof.Let S be a non-empty subset of ℝ that is bounded above. Then by the completeness property there exists ℓ∈ℝ such that ℓ=sup(S). Now consider the set S′={−s:s∈S}. It is clear that S′ is a non-empty subset of ℝ which is bounded below, and that −ℓ=inf(S). ∎

3.3.1 Intervals

Quite often we will need some compact notation for denoting a continuous subset of ℝ, such as the positive real numbers, all real numbers between -1 and 4, etc. These subsets are called intervals, and we will define them now.

Definition 3.2.

Let a,b∈ℝ with a<b. Then the closed interval from a to b is given by

[a,b]={x∈ℝ:a≤x≤b},

and the open interval from a to b is given by

(a,b)={x∈ℝ:a<x<b}.

Likewise the half-open intervals may be defined similarly as

(a,b]={x∈ℝ:a<x≤b},
[a,b)={x∈ℝ:a≤x<b}.

To denote a half-unbounded or fully unbounded interval, we can use a ∞ symbol. For example:

[a,∞)={x∈ℝ:a≤x},

or

(−∞,0)={x∈ℝ:x<0},

and we could also say

(−∞,∞)=ℝ.

It is also useful to note that

sup((a,b))=sup([a,b])=b,inf((a,b))=inf([a,b])=a

.
